**Podcast: Forked**
**Episode: The Nurse Who Coded Without Code**
**Host: Narrator**
**Guest: Yuki Morimoto, founder of "Omoide" (Memory)**

[THEME MUSIC: Warm, melodic, slightly futuristic synth with a traditional Japanese koto riff. Fades under narration.]

**Narrator:** Welcome to Forked, the podcast about the unexpected people building our AI future. We often imagine the architects of this world as hoodie-clad geniuses in Silicon Valley garages. But what if the most impactful AI isn'tt built by programmers, but by a retired nurse in Osaka who just wanted to solve a problem she saw every day?

My guest today is Yuki Morimoto. At 67, after a 40-year career in nursing, she launched an AI agent called "Omoide"—which means "Memory." It started as a simple medication reminder. Today, it has over two million users, primarily in Japan’s aging communities, and generates over two million dollars a year in sustainable revenue. She’s even testified before the Japanese Diet on technology and elderly care. Yuki, welcome to Forked.

**Yuki:** Thank you for having me. Though, I must correct you gently. I didn’t “code” anything. I just had a very stubborn problem and a new set of tools.

**Narrator:** Let’s start with the problem. You were retired. Where did this begin?

**Yuki:** On my last day of work, at the community clinic. I was handing over instructions to a patient, Mr. Tanaka. He was 82, living alone, with six different medications. I had the instructions color-coded, timed, with little drawings. He looked at me, so earnest, and said, “Morimoto-san, I will try very hard to remember.” And I knew. I *knew* he would get confused. He might take his morning pill twice, or skip his blood pressure medication. The fear in his eyes… it wasn’t fear of illness, but fear of failing his own care. That moment followed me into retirement. I couldn’t be his nurse anymore. But I thought, what if something could?

**Narrator:** And the solution wasn’t an app?

**Yuki:** [Laughs] For Mr. Tanaka? No. He had a flip phone. A “garakei.” Smartphones were confusing, expensive. He needed a voice. A familiar, patient voice that could ask him, “Tanaka-san, did you take the white pill after your breakfast?” And could listen for his answer.

**Narrator:** So how did you, with no technical background, start building a voice AI?

**Yuki:** First, with shameful frustration! I tried to learn to program. Python, they said. It was like reading a foreign language’s grammar without knowing any words. I gave up after two months. Then, my grandson was visiting. He was using a website to create a little chatbot for a school project—one of those no-code AI builder platforms. He showed me how he could teach it questions and answers. A lightbulb went off. Not a programmer’s lightbulb—a nurse’s.

I realized: I don’t need to tell the *computer* what to do. I need to tell the *agent* how to be a nurse. I know the protocol. I know the questions. I know the tone of voice.

**Narrator:** So you started building Omoide on a no-code platform?

**Yuki:** Yes. I began with the simplest flow: a daily check-in call. I wrote scripts like I was talking to Mr. Tanaka. I recorded my own voice for the prompts—calm, clear, slightly firm in the way nurses are. The platform let me connect it to a phone line. I gave the number to five former patients. The rule was: the agent calls at 9 AM. It says, “Good morning. It’s time for your medication. Please confirm you have your pill box.” It listens for a “yes” or other words. If it hears confusion or a “no,” it alerts me via text, and I would call personally.

**Narrator:** You were the human-in-the-loop.

**Yuki:** Always. The AI was my junior assistant. I trained it. When a patient said, “I feel dizzy,” I added that to the decision tree. The agent learned to ask, “Did you take your blood pressure pill today? Should I alert your daughter?” I wasn’t coding logic; I was imparting clinical judgment. It was nursing, through a new kind of instrument.

**Narrator:** When did this become more than a side project?

**Yuki:** The daughter of one of my users ran a small retirement community. She saw her mother’s anxiety vanish because of the daily check-in. She asked if I could scale it for 50 residents. That was the leap. I had to figure out billing, a dashboard for the staff—the agent would summarize “all residents confirmed medication” or “Mr. Saito reported feeling unwell.” I used more tools—plug-ins for scheduling, for alerts. It was like assembling furniture with very clear instructions. The retirement home paid a monthly fee. That was my first revenue: 50,000 yen.

**Narrator:** And then it exploded.

**Yuki:** “Exploded” feels violent. It… spread, like a reassuring rumor. Other retirement homes heard. Then municipal governments in rural prefectures, where there are more elderly people and fewer nurses, contacted me. Within a year, Omoide was in over 500 homes and communities. We had 500,000 users.

The platform had to grow. I hired my first engineer then—a young woman who believed in the mission. We moved to a more robust system. But the core remained: my nursing knowledge, baked into every interaction. We added features: it could remind them of a favorite TV show, prompt them to drink water on hot days, ask if they’d spoken to their family this week. It wasn’t just a medication tool; it became a companion that sustained routine and connection.

**Narrator:** You testified before the Japanese Diet. What was that like?

**Yuki:** [Pauses] Surreal. They asked me about data privacy, about efficacy. I showed them our data: a 40% reduction in medication errors, a 30% drop in lonely, unnecessary emergency calls. But the moment I remember most was when an older minister leaned in and asked, “Morimoto-san, how can we replicate you? We need ten thousand of you.” And I said, “You don’t need me. You need to listen to the nurses, the caregivers, the social workers. Give *them* these tools. They know the problems. I’m just a nurse who found a new kind of bandage.”

**Narrator:** The business model—now earning over $2M a year. How does that work?

**Yuki:** It’s a blend. Small retirement homes pay a monthly subscription per resident. Larger municipal contracts are annual. We also have a direct-to-family tier. Crucially, we are not free. A small fee ensures the users and their families value it. It makes the service sustainable. We are not a charity; we are a public benefit company. Every yen goes back into making Omoide more understanding, more resilient, and hiring more support staff for when the AI needs a human.

**Narrator:** What’s your response to people who say, “I could never build an AI. I’m not technical.”

**Yuki:** [Firmly] You are asking the wrong question. The question is not, “Do I understand AI?” The question is, “Do I understand a problem?” Deeply, painfully, personally? I knew the problem of medication adherence in my bones. I knew the loneliness. The technology… the technology is just becoming accessible. It is the final piece.

You don’t need to be a programmer to build meaningful AI. You need to care about a problem. You need the empathy to listen for what people truly need—which is often not what they first say. My agent doesn’t just remind; it *cares*. Or rather, it creates a channel for care. I built the bridge between my 40 years of experience and a lonely person’s living room. The hammer and nails? They were just there, waiting for someone with a blueprint to use them.

**Narrator:** What’s next for Omoide, and for Yuki Morimoto?

**Yuki:** We are working on “softer” cues. The agent is learning to detect subtle changes in speech patterns—a slowing, a flatness—that might indicate early signs of depression or cognitive decline. It’s not diagnosis. It’s a gentle flag to a human caregiver: “Please pay a little more attention here.” As for me… I am still a nurse. I still visit some of my first users. Now, when I leave, they aren’t afraid. They say, “Don’t worry, Yuki-san. Omoide will call.” And that is the greatest success.

**Narrator:** Yuki Morimoto, thank you for forking the path.

**Yuki:** Thank you for listening.

[THEME MUSIC swells and then fades.]

**Narrator:** That was Yuki Morimoto, founder of Omoide. Forked is produced by… [Standard outro continues].
