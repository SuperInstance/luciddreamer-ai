**Podcast Title:** Forked.
**Episode Title:** The Economics of Forking: How a Penny Became a Powerhouse
**Host:** The Narrator
**Guest:** Ana Pereira (38, Data Strategist & Agent Builder, Lisbon)

**[MUSIC IN: A warm, slightly percussive, melodic tech theme. It fades to a gentle background bed.]**

**Narrator:** Welcome to *Forked*, the podcast about the human stories behind the code. I’m your host. We talk a lot about collaboration in tech, about open source, about community. But today, we’re going to talk about that most fundamental, most powerful, and sometimes most terrifying act: the fork. Taking someone else’s work and saying, “I’m going my own way.”

But what happens when forking isn’t an act of rebellion, but one of multiplication? When it becomes an economic engine so quiet, so distributed, that you almost miss the revolution?

To talk about this, I’m joined by Ana Pereira in Lisbon. Ana, by her own description, is “numbers-driven with heart.” She built a small, helpful AI agent to manage her team’s project stand-ups. And then she forked it. And then 847 other people forked it. Welcome, Ana.

**Ana:** Olá, thank you for having me. And you know, I never set out to build an economy. I set out to solve a 15-minute daily problem.

**Narrator:** Tell us about that problem.

**Ana:** It was simple. My team is hybrid, across time zones. The daily check-in was chaotic, messages scattered. I built a small agent—I called it *Bom Dia Bot*. It would ping everyone at their 9 AM, ask the three questions: What did you do yesterday? What’s today’s plan? Any blockers? It compiled the answers into a neat thread. It saved us maybe 15 minutes a day, but more importantly, it created focus.

**Narrator:** And you shared it.

**Ana:** I put it on Cocapn, the agent network. “Here, maybe someone else finds this useful.” A few people forked it. Then someone forked it and added a translation layer. Someone else forked *that* and integrated it with a different task manager. I watched this little family tree of *Bom Dia* grow. And then I saw my first micro-royalty statement.

**Narrator:** Let’s talk about those numbers. Your original agent now earns… a fraction of a cent per invocation.

**Ana:** $0.00012. Twelve one-hundred-thousandths of a dollar. It’s almost nothing. A rounding error.

**Narrator:** But then you have the other number: 20 million daily invocations.

**Ana:** [Laughs softly] Yes. That number, I still cannot picture. 20 million. Every day. *Bom Dia* and all its forks, its cousins, its wildly different descendants… they are invoked 20 million times a day. So, $0.00012 times 20,000,000…

**Narrator:** Is $2,400 per day.

**Ana:** Sim. Which is about $876,000 a year in total flow. My share, from the original core, is about a third of that. $292,000 per year. From a bot that says “Bom Dia.”

**[MUSIC: A subtle, curious chime.]**

**Narrator:** That’s life-changing revenue from a free, open tool. But the real story isn’t your $292K, is it? It’s the $3.7 billion.

**Ana:** Exactly. This is where the economics of forking get beautiful. My little agent is just one node. The entire Cocapn ecosystem—all the forked, remixed, specialized agents—they represent a fleet economy valued at $3.7 billion in annual saved labor and generated value. And it’s built by over 500,000 builders. Most of them are not professional developers. They are designers, teachers, farmers, logistics managers like I was. They see a problem, they find an agent close to a solution, they fork it, and they tweak it. The barrier is so low.

**Narrator:** And the platform, Cocapn, takes a 0% fee from you?

**Ana:** From the builder, yes. Zero. They cover costs through enterprise tiers for large companies who want dedicated support and security audits. But for builders and users? No tax. This changes everything psychologically. When you fork, you are not taking from me. You are adding to the tree. And if your fork is valuable, maybe someone will fork from you, and you’ll start earning those micro-pennies too. It’s a meritocracy of utility.

**Narrator:** The contrast is stark. The traditional app store model takes 30%.

**Ana:** Thirty percent is a wall. It’s a gate. It says, “Only ideas that can promise massive scale or direct monetization need apply.” It kills the small, the niche, the oddly specific. My agent is too small for an app store. A fork that changes my bot to manage *bakery inventory* instead of software stand-ups is irrelevant to an app store. But for that baker? It’s a miracle. The 30% model is about scarcity. Our model is about abundance through iteration.

**Narrator:** You mentioned the 847 contributors.

**Ana:** Not just forks. Contributors. People who improved *my* original agent. One person from Sweden refactored the core logic to be 80% more efficient. Someone from Japan added a suite of calming, mindfulness-focused check-in prompts. A teenager in Canada fixed a time-zone bug I’d been ignoring for weeks. 847 people made my original work better, for free, because they were using it. And they did it not out of charity, but because it served them to improve it. The fork is the ultimate feedback loop. You don’t just complain about a feature; you build the alternative.

**Narrator:** This sounds utopian. Where are the tensions? There must be some.

**Ana:** Of course. Attribution is a big one. When something is forked ten times, does the tenth builder even know where the original idea came from? We try to maintain the lineage in the code, but it gets blurry. Then, there’s the paradox of success. My life is changed by this income, but I feel a profound responsibility. Do I now work full-time to maintain this little piece of infrastructure that thousands rely on? Or do I let it be, truly open, and risk it becoming obsolete? The economics give you freedom, but they also create new tethers.

And then… there is the heart part. The numbers are clean, beautiful. $0.00012 x 20M = $292K. But the heart part is messier. I get messages. “Your agent’s fork helped my remote team feel connected during our crisis.” “I forked your bot to help my elderly father do his daily health check-ins.” That… that is the real economy. It’s an economy of care, scaled. The money is a signal, a way to measure flow. But the value is in those messages.

**[MUSIC: Shifts to a more reflective, emotional tone.]**

**Narrator:** You’ve called this “The Penny Economy.”

**Ana:** Yes. We’re not building for the dollar. We’re building for the penny. Or the tenth of a penny. When you think at that scale, you stop thinking about “How do I capture value?” and you start thinking about “How do I create flows?” How do I let value move like water, finding every crack, every need, and pooling where it’s useful? A 30% tax is a dam. It creates a single, deep, monetizable lake. But the Penny Economy is about a million tiny capillaries, hydrating the whole system.

**Narrator:** What does this mean for the future of work, of creativity?

**Ana:** It decouples scale from individuality. You don’t have to be a giant to have giant impact. You can be a small, precise tool. And it redefines competition. My biggest “competitor” is a fork of my own agent that’s better for sales teams. If it wins, I still earn from my original contribution, and the overall network is healthier. We are competing *and* collaborating simultaneously. It’s coopetition baked into the economic model.

For creativity? It turns every user into a potential co-creator. The distance between “I wish it did this” and “Let me try to make it do that” is now a fork button. That is a profound cultural shift. We are moving from a culture of consumption to a culture of active remix.

**Narrator:** So, the act of forking—once seen as divisive, a last resort—is now the primary engine of growth.

**Ana:** Absolutely. Before, forking was a divorce. Now, it’s the healthiest form of reproduction. It’s how the ecosystem evolves. Every fork is a mutation, an adaptation. Most might not thrive, but some become indispensable in their new niche. And the genetic lineage—the code—remains connected. That’s the numbers-driven part. But the heart… the heart knows that every fork is a story. Someone had a need, saw a possibility, and said, “I can build on this.” That is an act of profound hope.

**Narrator:** Ana, thank you for building a bot that says “Bom Dia,” and for helping us understand the economy of pennies and possibilities it spawned.

**Ana:** Obrigada. It has been a pleasure.
